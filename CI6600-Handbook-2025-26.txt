	•	 Introduction
The overall aim of the module i to further develop skills in organisation, timekeeping, research literature, developing and critically analysing results as well as reporting work verbally and in a written format. The end result will be an artefact or artefacts which demonstrates creativity and technical competence as well as a technical report.

This module provides you with an opportunity to self-direct, manage and complete your own unique project related to your degree subject. This is your project to develop, manage and conclude. You can start to develop your chosen area by selecting a project, type which will help you with the project proposal assessment.

Project Types and Themes:
	•	A review-based project where the aims focus on comparisons of functions, bene-fits and limitations of tools, techniques, apps, assets, networks, or systems, that are contextualised in a use case or scenario.

	•	An investigation-based project where the aims focus on a combination of discov-ery, exposure, acquisition, analysis, visualisation and documentation of typically unchartered knowledge and information.

	•	A proof of concept-based project where aims focus on building or designing an artefact that solves or mitigates a problem, within the context of a use case or scenario.

	•	 Project Management
For your final year, you need to organise your time effectively, even more so than in previous years. Remember that you are attending lectures and working on assignments for other modules as well as your Project. Therefore, it is important to realise that project work should not be allowed to interfere with examinations and other module assessments. Moreover, you should not allow other academic work to take all your time; you must continue to work on your Project for the minimum recommended amount of time (10 hours every week, all through the year).

Please consider deadlines from other modules on your GANNT chart or work plan, so that the other activities are incorporated in your planning.

You can discover more about project management via LinkedIn Learning and O’Reilly Media.

	•	Guidance on completing the project report (60%)

The project report is the final submission for this module and is the main assessment task of the module. Some general guidelines are given below but ultimately production of your report is your own responsibility. You should of course discuss as much as possible with your supervisor along the way.

The written presentation is important. It is essential to carefully proof-read your report and, if possible, to get someone else to check it. Your supervisor will advise you on your presentation, but you should ensure that he/she is given adequate time to look at your draft copy for comment and correction.

The regulations for the assessment of the project report do not specify a maximum size but the final report should be a maximum of 6000- 8000 words in length. Please try not to exceed 8000 words. This is just a guideline, and it does not include mathematical equations, computer output, source code (in the appendix), tabulated data etc.

The report must be typed on A4 paper with 1 ½ spacing, 2.54 cm margin on the left- and right-hand side. With the exception of the title page, the glossary of terms and the contents page, each page must be numbered. You are responsible for the typing, preparation, and submission of the project report.

You must use the university standard front page as the first page of your report, see Appendix 2. An indicative project report structure is provided at the end of this document, see Appendix 3.

The report is submitted electronically as a Word or PDF document through Canvas and through the TurnItIn website. A compressed folder with your artefact needs to be uploaded onto Canvas as well. A late submission will be downgraded unless a mitigating circumstances claim has been submitted prior to the submission deadline.
IMPORTANT: A computer “breakdown” or loss of computer output is not normally regarded as a valid excuse.

The project report will be assessed by your supervisor and an appointed second examiner. All the projects will also be seen by the External Examiner. The precise assessment criteria are described in the project marking scheme shown in the next section.

	•	Viva (15%)
The viva assesses the final achievement and an understanding of the subject matter. During the viva session, you will need to present a summary of your project (No more than 10 minutes). A summary presentation (e.g., PowerPoint) based on the structure of your project report needs to be prepared, which should aim to answer the following questions:

	•	What is the theme of your project?
	•	Why is it of interest or important?
	•	Who are your project stakeholders and how will the outcomes of your project impact them?
	•	What methodology did you choose and why?
	•	What were the results of your project and what did you learn or discover, that you did not know when you started?
	•	What are the next steps for your project if it were to continue?
	•	What would you do differently if you had the opportunity to start your project again?

You must also present a demonstration of your artefact (no more than 10 minutes). A practical demonstration of what you have created, designed, built or results.

Be prepared to answer questions from your examiners, about the material presented in the project report and about your artefact.

The above requirements and procedures are subject to review. Should any changes be implemented you will be notified.
	•	 Plagiarism

Key skills developed during project work are your self-reliance, originality, and initiative as well as your ability to collect, assimilate and utilise knowledge / information. In doing so you should take care to avoid plagiarism; claiming someone else’s work as your own. Whether it is the work of another student or extracted from a publication, the author should be cited. This will not detract from the authenticity of your project; it may add to its technical credibility. Failure to cite authors and making it clear whether a section is a quotation from another work will be classed as plagiarism and is likely to be treated as Academic Misconduct, which carries severe penalties.

Plagiarism is a serious academic offence and will always be challenged where it is suspected. When submitting work for assessment ensure it is your own or your group’s and not someone else’s ideas, words, and images. You must declare that the task submitted for assessment is your own or group’s independent work and that all sources used in the submission are referenced. 	•	Proposal and progress mark

The mark for the development work will be determined as a result of taking into account the following factors:

	•	Your understanding of the problem
	•	The planning of the project
	•	The effort you put in
	•	Your self-reliance
	•	The originality and initiative shown
	•	The way you have organised the work
	•	Your week-by-week performance
	•	The project logs
	•	Prototype demonstration


Guidelines:

A mark of 70% and above would normally require a high standard of performance for each of the above factors.

A mark between 60% and 70% would normally require a very good performance for each of the above factors with rather less originality and initiative shown.

A mark between 50% and 60%) would normally require a good performance for the majority of the above factors but with little or no originality and self-reliance.

A mark between 40% and 50% would normally require an adequate performance in most respects but without showing originality, self-reliance and initiative.

A mark of less than 40% would arise in the case of very little effort and understanding, inability to achieve the minimum objectives of the project, or fatal flaws in the analysis or methodology and inadequate project logs and viva. A satisfactory performance in some of the work and adequate effort, at least for some of the time may warrant an unclassified performance.

	•	Artefact and report mark

Different type of projects requires different treatment and lend themselves to demonstration of different skills. The following factors will be examined (if relevant) in defining the content mark:

	•	Your understanding of the subject matter
	•	The clarity of communication to the reader
	•	Rigour of analysis and design
	•	Depth and/or breadth of implementation
	•	Evidence of originality, imagination, critical judgement, ingenuity
	•	Legal, Ethical and Social and Data Security Considerations

Guidelines:

A mark of 70% and above would normally require an outstanding, comprehensive treatment for each part of the contents described above, with outstanding analysis. The report should show a complete grasp of the problem, the achievement of its aims and it should reach a satisfactory well-argued conclusion.

A mark between 60% and 70% would normally require a very good thorough treatment for each part of the contents with good quality analysis. The report should show a very good understanding of the problem, the achievement of its aims, with rather less originality and initiative than a first-class performance, and it should reach a satisfactory, reasonably well-argued conclusion.

A mark between 50% and 60% would normally require a good satisfactory treatment for each part of the contents with adequate analysis. The report should show a good understanding of the problem, the achievement of the majority of its aims but with little or no originality and it should reach an adequate conclusion.

A mark between 40% and 50% would normally be adequate in its treatment of the parts of the contents with adequate analysis. The report should show that the student has understood the problem to a large extent, has achieved some of its aims and reached an adequate conclusion.

A mark below 40% would arise in the case of very little effort, inability to achieve the minimum objectives of the project or fatal flaws in the analysis or methodology. The project should have displayed an inadequate level of understanding. A partial understanding of the problem and an incomplete solution may warrant consideration for an unclassified performance.

	•	Viva and interim demos mark

This mark will reflect your performance in the following factors:

	•	Oral presentation and viva
	•	Organisation of the presentation
	•	Logical structure
	•	Clarity of expression
	•	Comprehension of subject matter

Guidelines:

A mark of 70% and above would normally require a high standard of performance for each of the factors mentioned in the brackets above.

A mark between 60% and 70% would normally require a very good performance for each of the factors mentioned.

A mark between 50% and 60% would normally require a satisfactory performance for all the above factors and a good performance for the majority.

A mark between 40% and 50%) would normally be adequate in most respects but unsatisfactory for some of the factors.

A mark below 40% would arise in the case of a very poor presentation, difficult to follow, badly written with many errors and omissions. A poor presentation which is satisfactory in some respects may warrant an unclassified performance.
